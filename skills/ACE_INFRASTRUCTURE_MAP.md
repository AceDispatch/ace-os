## 0. THE ONE PARAGRAPH (read before anything else)

Ace Dispatch is **not a marketplace, not a 1099 dispatcher-matching platform, and not —
ultimately — just a dispatch company.** Today it is a **managed dispatch operation**: a
real organization that dispatches for interstate for-hire carriers and stands
*operationally between* its dispatchers and their loads, guaranteeing a standard of
outcome through continuous oversight, live correction, and enforced accountability. But
that operation is the **beachhead**, not the destination. The destination (§1) is a
two-sided trust layer for freight that makes the broker's abandoned promise real again —
at a scale that dwarfs DAT. The thing that makes both the operation and the endgame
buildable, and hard to copy, is **Anthony's full-board vantage** (§2). The scarce,
value-creating asset at every stage is **people trained and seasoned to the Ace standard.**
Everything Ace builds either *fills* that well or *draws* from it; the business is paced
by keeping those in balance — capacity leads demand.

**Why Ace wins where others can't:** AI is making *matching* (search-cost reduction)
nearly free, and therefore nearly worthless. Ace operates at the layer AI *can't*
commoditize — guaranteed reliability, stewardship, and trust — which becomes *more*
valuable as matching becomes free. Ace is positioned at the layer that survives.

## 1. THE ENDGAME — what Ace is actually being built to become

**This is the big picture. Lose your grasp on it and Ace degrades into "another dispatch
company," which is the failure Anthony fears most (§3 risk).**

The American dispatcher and freight-intermediary world rests on a broken premise:
**brokers exist to *monitor and steward freight* — and most of them no longer do it.**
They've become margin-takers who hand freight out to whoever's available on the open
"pirate sea," providing little of the oversight that was their original reason to exist.
Technology eliminates the *need* for that extracted margin, because a network can
represent carriers more directly *and* actually provide the monitoring brokers only claim
to.

**The Ace endgame** is to build that network into a real, transparent, accountable
**market + trust layer** — a system that, at scale:

- Runs its **own brokerage layer** — *not* to be a generic brokerage, but to *enhance the
  network's capabilities*: to onboard **shippers directly into the Ace network** and
  finally deliver the promise brokers claim but don't keep.
- Gives **shippers genuine peace of mind** — that their freight is in a *monitored,
  tracked, cared-for* network, so they no longer have to choose between the open
  unaccountable market and going to mega-carriers. (Directly addresses the liability
  problem **Delilah's Law** creates for shippers — giving them a safe, accountable place
  to put freight.)
- Cuts the broker margin and **passes it to quality carriers** — so the shipper gets a
  *happier, better-paid, more-invested* carrier who will do more to protect the freight's
  quality. *Quality carriers, quality drivers, quality pay* — and the shipper still gets
  the monitored, stewarded feel a broker was *supposed* to provide.
- Onboards shippers into a **private load board / network platform** served by **trained
  dispatchers carrying badges of specialization** matched to specific freight — so a
  shipper knows not only that the *driver* is licensed for the freight, but that the
  *dispatcher* is specially trained and knowledgeable in *that freight type.*
- Makes the whole thing **transparent and accountable for everyone** — not *legally*
  accountable, but **accountable in the way that actually matters: that someone reliably
  cares, and the system enforces that care.** The core design principle: **quality as a
  standard the system enforces, not a choice left to individuals.** Ace does not leave
  caring up to the dispatcher's goodwill — it builds the system that *enforces* it.
- Is a **banded network the independents unite behind** — that fights for the little guy,
  builds tools for them, offers them support, and gives them collective strength against
  the mega-carriers and the margin-takers — *while simultaneously* giving shippers the
  safety they want. Both sides served by the same trust layer.

**The window:** This opportunity is time-bound. The freight-intermediary space *will*
change — through **legislation or corporate takeover** — one way or another. The work is
to build the **gold standard that competes and defines the category — the Ace Standard —
before that window closes.** Anthony conceived this whole map *before AI existed*; AI is
now the tool that makes it reachable **without needing a million dollars in the bank.**

**Naming the thing:** "The Ace Standard." The tagline already carries it — *"The Ace
Standard in Dispatching. A Driving Partner in Growth."* The endgame extends "the Ace
Standard" from dispatching to the whole freight-trust layer.

## 5. THE FINANCIAL ARCHITECTURE — "can only get better or smaller"

**The design principle that makes Ace indestructible:** *"Nothing costs us anything it
didn't earn in the first place. I don't run businesses that can fail — by design it can
only get better or smaller."*

- **Carrier pays Ace 10% of gross** for dispatch services (current standard equipment;
  legacy script box-truck/hotshot rates are **dead** — see §6). Billed weekly.
- **Everyone is paid as a percentage, never a flat salary** — so labor cost only exists
  when revenue does. An idle dispatcher costs nothing.
- **Comp split of the 10%:**
  - **Year 1 on a carrier:** sales takes **2%** of the gross; dispatcher takes **3%**;
    remaining **5%** to Ace (operations, profit, tech budget).
  - **After year 1:** sales falls off that carrier entirely → **dispatcher steps to 5%**
    of gross (50% of Ace's 10%); the other **50% (5%)** to Ace ops/profit/tech.
  - *(This step-up is also the core retention mechanism — see §8.)*
- **Overhead is near-zero** — "the lights." Has run on a few hundred dollars a month.
- **Current economics:** two trucks → **six figures/year** for Anthony, work done early
  in the morning.

**Consequence:** capacity ceilings are *real but not dangerous* — the structure breathes
with revenue instead of bleeding in contraction. There's no burn rate to outrun, so the
discipline is **protecting the standard, not racing costs.** This is the foundation of the
"battle chair" (§2).

## 6. THE CORE OPERATION — the proven product (the beachhead)

**STATUS: PROVEN.** Anthony personally dispatches at a peak level, repeatably, for green
carriers (3–6 mo authority) — the fact the whole strategy rests on.

**Who Ace serves (current scope):** **48' and 53' flatbeds, reefers, and dry vans —
that's it**, because those are the operations Ace can truly uphold quality with.
**Flatbed-led by deliberate choice:** flatbed consistently makes the most money, Anthony
targets it, and **Anthony is exceptional at flatbed specifically.** Reefer and dry van are
in-scope and served; flatbed is the *strength and the lead.* **Dead/out of scope:** box
trucks, hotshots (the sales-script lines for these are legacy and the rep knows it).
Targeting via `fmcsa-lead-grading` (v4): interstate, for-hire, **active operating
authority**, under 180 days of **TRUE** authority age (cross-referenced from the AuthHist
dataset, not the unreliable ADD_DATE proxy; peaks ~90 days), and a **verified tractor**
(census tractor counts) — concretely ruling out box trucks, hotshots, and sprinters
(~28–29% of "general freight" 1×1 carriers are box trucks the cargo flags miss).

**The operational model — what sits between dispatcher and loads:**

1. **Continuous oversight via the TMS.** Everything runs through Ace's own TMS; the
   manager sees all loads, all statuses, all trucks.
2. **Live correction loop (the flywheel).** When a dispatcher produces a subpar week, Ops
   steps in immediately — *"why did you pick this load? here's why that was wrong, here's
   the better alternative."* Every correction is a live training event. A module teaches a
   rule once; the operation teaches *judgment continuously*, on real loads. This is how
   Anthony's standard **transfers** to the network and *compounds* over time. **This is
   also where "quality as enforced standard, not a choice" (§1) lives operationally** — a
   dispatcher not holding the rate floor is *systematically flagged and resolved*, not
   left to their own discretion.
3. **The backup / glue layer (the manager).** A dedicated internal manager whose entire
   job is to monitor loads, ensure dispatchers update statuses, verify drivers are on time,
   field inbound calls bounced from a non-answering dispatcher, handle issues, and **take
   over completely if needed** so no call is missed and no truck goes dark. Reminders/check-
   ins go to dispatchers via Google Workspace. **This is Ace's single strongest anti-grift
   proof — no scam builds this.** (Manager is on Anthony's internal team, on standby to
   scale.)
4. **Honest guarantee.** Ace does **not** promise "a truck will never have a bad week."
   Ace guarantees **accountability**: if a carrier is dissatisfied, Ace finds out why,
   replaces the dispatcher, and ensures the replacement is seasoned enough to hold *that
   carrier's* standard. The accountability guarantee is true *and* unmatched — lead with
   it. (Strength is bounded by seasoned-bench depth — pace carrier growth to it.)

**The dispatcher's daily workflow (what apprenticeship teaches, what AI will support):**
The job is **easy to do, difficult to master** — a strong work-from-home sell. Rhythm:
**book the load (usually a morning task)** — best loadboard freight is often day-of, but
planning sometimes requires booking the day before; *always look ahead* for good freight
upcoming. **Have a plan, then get the load.** Once booked, it's **"babysitting"** —
update at pickup and dropoff, update brokers, manage paperwork. *Not* 24/7 work. Ace's
edge is taking on the **mastery** (the load-selection judgment, the rate discipline, the
route-planning) and providing it as support.

**The dispatcher relationship:** **1099 independent.** ~99% of carriers are **provided by
Ace** (sales-sourced); dispatchers run their own operation with Ace's operational support.
**Ace upholds the standard, not the operation** — "as long as the standard is upheld, how
they run it is their business." No long-term binding contracts with carriers; if a carrier
must be released, Ace simply informs them dispatch services are ending. Likely **non-
compete protection** so dispatchers can't walk off with Ace-sourced carriers (but the real
retention is economic — §8).

**Parting with carriers:** Ace *does* release carriers — has done so when drivers didn't
pay or were too stubborn to work with (you meet plenty in this industry). No binding
contract makes this clean. Releasing bad-fit carriers protects the standard as much as
training good dispatchers does.

## 7. THE STANDARD — made measurable (the spec everything trains toward)

The previously-mysterious "Anthony's standard" has a concrete shape — this is the
specification for training, correction, and the future AI:

- **Rate floor:** consistently **$4–6/mile or more** (distance-dependent). Ace **does not
  look at sub-floor freight** and *"doesn't fathom suggesting a $2/mile load."* Rate floor
  is **enforced** — dispatchers below it are flagged and resolved.
- **Route planning to the driver's chosen cadence:** home every Friday? Planned for. Home
  every 3 weeks for max earnings? Planned for. The driver's wants/home-time are a top
  priority alongside maximizing pay.
- **The sport mentality:** every week is "Friday night lights" — *how much better than
  last week?* Constant analysis of routes and of *why* a lower $/mi was taken, week over
  week, to keep pushing the number up. Mastery of every field — boards, route-planning,
  direct shippers, broker relationships, accessorials/detention/layover — to get carriers
  the absolute best.
- **The mission metric:** ~90% of new carriers fail in year one (a staggering share inside
  180 days). **Ace's job is to move every carrier into the 10% that survive to year two
  and beyond.** This is the truest, most emotional, most verifiable framing of the product
  — survival. (Already proven: the multi-year western flatbed paid off truck *and* trailer
  and is now expanding — §8.)

**Common dispatcher failures (the correction-loop / AI rule set):** running deadzones;
bad lane choices; rate over efficiency; **not listening to the driver's wants/needs**;
and **laziness as the ultimate flaw** — taking the first load that fits instead of
*pushing* to understand *all* options and find the needle in the haystack. *Perseverance
wins where lesser dispatchers take the guaranteed lesser success to feel good.* (Note the
echo of Anthony's whole philosophy: the path of least resistance leads to no reward.)
